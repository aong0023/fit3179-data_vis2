Shark Incidents

Total Shark Incidents In Australia

This map shows the total number of shark incidents recorded
along Australian beaches. It highlights regions with higher incidents
for better awareness and safety planning.

Tip: Select a year at the bottom on the selection menu to analyse each year's data.

Insights

  • High incident clusters coincide with popular swimming and surfing beaches, especially along NSW and southern QLD.
  • Western and eastern coasts dominate the dataset, showing similar seasonal peaks in incident rates.
  • Human activity levels are the strongest predictor of incident frequency.
  • Data suggests that densely populated coasts, such as Sydney and Perth regions, experience more encounters.
  • Fewer incidents in Northern Territory and South Australia reflect lower recreational beach use.
  • Useful for safety planning: Helps policymakers, lifeguards, and local councils identify high-risk zones.

Shark Incidents by State

This area chart represents the number of shark incidents by
state over time, helping to identify trends and patterns across Australia's
coastline.

Tip: Click items in the legend to isolate a state. Click again to show all.
Hover over the lines to see the exact values.

Insights

  • New South Wales typically shows the highest incident counts among states.
  • Northern Territory and Tasmania generally record the fewest incidents.
  • States with the most people in the water see more incidents. NSW and WA have long stretches of easily accessiable surf beaches near major cities, such as Sydney and Perth. Due to year-round surfing and heavy tourism, shark-human contact opportunities are simply higher.

Beaches in Australia

Beach Slopes per Coastal Region in Australia

This bar chart illustrates the slopes of beaches in different
coastal regions. Steeper beaches may influence the frequency or nature
of shark encounters.

Tip: Hover over the bars to see the details and pick a state at the filter section.

Insights

  • Western Australia tends to have steep beaches because of the strong Indian Ocean wave that erode the coast.
  • Queensland and New South Whales have moderate slopes due to steady wave action balanced by high sediment supply from rivers.
  • Tasmania and South Australia have gentle beaches because of calmer waters and fine sediments that create low-gradient shores.
  • Coarser sand forms steeper beaches, while finer sand leads to flatter, more gradual ones.
